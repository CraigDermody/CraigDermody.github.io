title: "Make the Right Choice the Easy Choice"
date: 2026-02-11

A lot of government processes are designed as though the hard part is persuading people. If residents are not enrolling, responding, showing up, or completing a form, the instinct is often to add more explanation, more outreach, more warnings, or more instructions.

Sometimes that helps. Often it does not.

In many cases, the real problem is not a lack of information. It is friction. The process is too easy to ignore, too hard to understand, poorly timed, or too annoying to finish. That is where [behavioral insights](https://oecd-opsi.org/guide/behavioural-insights/) are useful, not as a clever communications tactic, but as a practical way to design public services around how people actually behave.

That distinction matters. In local government, it is easy to treat behavioral insights as a niche topic, something adjacent to communications or innovation. I think that framing misses the point. At its best, this work is just disciplined service improvement. It asks a simple question: where, exactly, are people getting stuck, and what would make the desired action easier to take?

One reason this gets missed is that public agencies are usually too familiar with their own systems. Staff know the acronyms. They know which letter matters and which one can be ignored. They know how to interpret a notice that feels obvious only because they have seen it a hundred times. Residents do not have that advantage. They are encountering the service in the middle of work, childcare, financial stress, limited language access, or simple distraction. A process that looks straightforward from inside government can feel confusing or risky from the outside.

I saw the value of these ideas while working on performance improvement projects for CleanPowerSF, San Francisco’s municipal power service. CleanPowerSF is cheaper than PG&E generation and uses cleaner sources, but that alone does not explain its popularity.

The underlying policy design uses one of the most effective nudges: the [default effect](https://www.bi.team/publications/mindspace/). By law and program design, CleanPowerSF is opt out. Eligible customers are automatically enrolled unless they take action to return to PG&E generation service. In practice, that default matters enormously. A public packet prepared for the San Francisco Local Agency Formation Commission described one cohort enrollment with a 99 percent participation rate, or about a 1 percent opt out rate. Even if you set aside the exact percentage, the lesson is the same: a well-chosen default can shape behavior at scale without forcing anyone’s hand.

That example is useful because it shows both the power and the limits of behavioral design. The default did real work. It reduced the burden on customers to make an active choice about a service they may not have had the time or context to evaluate closely. But it also worked because the underlying offer was defensible. CleanPowerSF was not using behavioral design to steer people into a bad outcome. The program offered a cleaner product at a competitive price, and the default helped translate that value into participation.

That is an important test for public-sector use of behavioral insights. If the underlying service is weak, confusing, or not in the public interest, a better nudge will not solve the real problem. It may only hide it for a while.

In practice, the most useful behavioral work usually starts with diagnosis rather than tactics. Before changing language, reminders, or defaults, it helps to identify the actual bottleneck. Are people failing to notice the communication at all? Are they postponing a task that feels tedious? Are they worried about making a costly mistake? Is the process asking for documents people do not have handy? Is a digital form hard to complete on a phone? These are different problems, and they call for different fixes.

That is why I tend to trust simple interventions more than flashy ones. Clearer action steps. Shorter notices. Better timing. Fewer fields. Better defaults. Reminders sent when a person can still act on them. In service delivery, small design choices compound. A resident does not experience policy, communications, forms, eligibility rules, and channel design as separate systems. They experience one task that either feels manageable or it does not.

There is also a measurement lesson here. Behavioral interventions are often presented as wins because one metric moves, but that is not enough. A change that increases enrollment may also increase confusion, complaint volume, or downstream staff workload. A simpler notice may improve response rates for one group while leaving language access barriers unresolved for another. Good measurement means looking at the whole service, not just the top line number you hoped would improve.

A few common mistakes come up repeatedly. One is starting with a favorite idea before identifying the real point of friction. Another is bundling too many changes together, which makes it hard to learn what actually worked. A third is treating behavioral design as harmless by definition. It is not. Public trust matters. People should still understand what is happening, what their choices are, and how to opt out or seek help.

The broader lesson is not that every government program needs a behavioral insights strategy. It is that many public-sector problems become easier to improve once you stop assuming that more information is the answer. Sometimes it is. Often, the better question is whether the service is asking too much attention, too much confidence, or too much effort from people who have very little to spare.

That is why I think behavioral insights belong squarely inside the practical craft of public service design. Find the friction. Respect the user. Make the next step obvious. Measure what changes. Then decide whether the improvement is real.
