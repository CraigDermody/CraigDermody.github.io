Local government services are often designed as if people are rational, attentive, and consistent. Read the notice, understand the instructions, take the right action, meet the deadline.

That’s not how residents move through their week. People are busy. They’re juggling childcare, work schedules, health issues, and a pile of mail they didn’t ask for. They start an application, hit a confusing step, and leave it for later. Later becomes never.

Behavioral insights starts from that reality. It is a practical way to improve services by paying attention to the small frictions that quietly decide outcomes. The wording of a notice. The order of steps. The default option. The timing of a reminder. When a program underperforms, you often don’t need new legislation or a major budget increase. You need to make the right action easier to take.

In local government, performance is full of moments like this: renewing a permit, showing up to an appointment, completing a benefits application, paying a fine, responding to a notice. These aren’t policy debates. They are service experiences. And they usually fail in predictable places.

A common failure mode is assuming that information changes behavior. If people aren’t complying, the thinking goes, they must not understand. So the response is more detail: longer letters, more web pages, more disclaimers.

But the bottleneck is often not knowledge. It is behavior under constraints.

The patterns show up everywhere:
- Too much cognitive load: long forms, dense language, too many decisions in one sitting.
- Ambiguity: residents can’t tell what “complete” means or what happens next.
- Present bias: “I’ll do it later” turns into “I missed it.”
- Hassle factors: logins, document requests, unclear eligibility, steps that only work on desktop.
- Fear and tone: punitive framing makes people disengage instead of comply.

Staff don’t always see these problems because staff live in the system. They know the acronyms. They know which documents count. They know what to do when a step breaks. Residents don’t. So they do what anyone does when a process feels confusing or high risk: they stop.

When programs underperform, it’s tempting to reach for enforcement or bigger incentives. Sometimes that’s necessary. But often the higher leverage move is simpler: redesign the service so success is the default, not an obstacle course.

A “nudge” is a practical tool within behavioral insights. The most famous example is the default option, what happens if you do nothing. Defaults are powerful because many people go with the flow, which gives the designer of the process immense influence.

In local government, the process of creating a nudge is less about clever psychology and more about disciplined service improvement:

1) **Understand the service as it exists today.**  
Map the resident journey from awareness to intent to action to completion. Include drop off points and failure states.

2) **Identify behavioral bottlenecks.**  
Where do people abandon? Where do they make avoidable errors? Where do they call for help? Where do they do nothing?

3) **Design alternative service versions.**  
Change the choice architecture. Simplify, clarify, reorder steps, set helpful defaults, adjust timing, reduce friction.

4) **Test the alternatives.**  
Use A/B tests, phased rollouts, or randomized trials when possible. Define success metrics before you launch.

5) **Scale what works and understand what didn’t.**  
Behavioral insights is a learning discipline. The point is to measure, learn, and improve.

This is also where ethics matters. Nudges should preserve autonomy and transparency. They should not rely on shame, deception, or dark patterns. The goal is not manipulation. The goal is making public services easier to use and more effective for the people they are meant to serve.

If you want behavioral insights to be more than a buzzword, you need a practical playbook: how to find bottlenecks, what to try, and how to measure success.

### Map the service journey with real data and observations

Start by mapping the end to end journey, but do it pragmatically. Most programs already have signals:

- Web analytics (abandonment rate, time on task, page exit points)
- Call center logs (top reasons for calls)
- Form error rates and incomplete submissions
- Appointment no show rates
- Renewal and compliance timelines
- Qualitative feedback from frontline staff

The point is to locate the true friction points, not to brainstorm interventions in the abstract.

### Use common “nudge patterns” that fit government services

Most successful nudges are not exotic. They are variations on a few patterns:

- **Simplification:** shorter, clearer notices; fewer steps; checklists; plain language.
- **Salience:** move the action step and deadline to the top; emphasize what matters now.
- **Defaults:** pre-filled forms, automatic enrollment or renewal where appropriate and lawful.
- **Timely prompts:** reminders that arrive when action is needed, not weeks too early.
- **Social norms:** “Most people in your area have already done X.”
- **Implementation intentions:** prompts that help people make a plan (“When will you do this?”).
- **Friction reduction:** fewer clicks, mobile friendly design, fewer documents up front.

These are especially powerful when paired with testing. The IRS, for example, has published a Behavioral Insights Toolkit that frames the work as identifying behavioral factors and then testing interventions, supported by analytics methods and measurable outcomes.

### Define success metrics around the problem, not the task

Pick metrics that measure the behavior you are trying to change, not just bottleneck itself. Common examples include:

- **Completion rate** (forms, renewals, applications)
- **Abandonment rate** and **error rate**
- **Time to complete** (or time to payment)
- **Appointment no show rate**
- **Call volume** for “how do I…?” questions
- **Equity slices** (do improvements help all groups equally?)

Pre-register your success criteria. If you decide what “worked” after the fact, you invite wishful thinking.

### Examples of successful nudges in public services

**1) Social norm tax letters (including local tax collection).**  
Field experiments in tax reminder letters have shown that adding social norm or public services messages can increase compliance. Hallsworth and colleagues found measurable improvements from norm based messages, with one of the most successful messages producing a treatment effect around five percentage points.
Behavioral Insights Team has also published work on applying norm messaging to local government tax collection.

**2) Home energy reports using neighbor comparisons.**  
Utilities have used social norm feedback (“you used more/less than similar households”) to reduce energy use. J-PAL summarizes evidence that these reports reduced consumption by about 2% across multiple utilities.  
This is a useful local government adjacent example because it demonstrates a core idea: residents do not need a lecture. They need a feedback design that makes the behavior change easier.

**3) Appointment reminders to reduce no shows.**  
Reminders (SMS, phone, mail) are among the most practical nudges in public health and social services. A review in the medical literature found that most studies reported improved attendance when reminders were used. Local governments can apply the same pattern to clinics, benefits offices, and inspections: reduce missed appointments, reduce wasted capacity, and improve outcomes without changing eligibility rules.

**4) Templates and tested letters used by local authorities.**  
Some local government associations publish ready to use behavioral templates and communications examples (for example, outreach to increase screening uptake or reduce non urgent calls) that reflect the broader pattern: diagnose friction, redesign the message, and track results. 

### Personal experience

I saw the power of these ideas while working on performance improvement projects for CleanPowerSF, San Francisco’s municipal power service. CleanPowerSF’s service is cheaper than PG&E generation and uses cleaner sources, but that alone does not fully explain its popularity.

The underlying policy design uses one of the most effective nudges: the default. By law and program design, CleanPowerSF is opt-out. Eligible customers are automatically enrolled unless they take action to return to PG&E generation service.

In practice, that default matters enormously. A public packet prepared for the San Francisco Local Agency Formation Commission described a cohort enrollment with a 99% participation rate (about a 1% opt out rate). Even if you set aside the exact percentage, the lesson is the same: a well chosen default can shape behavior at scale without forcing anyone’s hand.

This is exactly the kind of behavioral insight Thaler emphasizes: defaults are powerful because people tend to accept what is set for them unless they have a strong reason to switch. In local government, that power should be used carefully and transparently. But when it aligns with public value, cleaner power, simpler enrollment, lower bills, it can be a high impact, low friction way to deliver better outcomes.

Behavioral insights work can fail for predictable reasons.

**Starting with a favorite nudge instead of diagnosing the bottleneck.**  
If you begin with “let’s send a text message” or “let’s rewrite the letter,” you may miss the real barrier (eligibility confusion, document burden, fear of consequences).

**Testing without clean metrics or changing too many things at once.**  
If you redesign a letter, a website, and the process simultaneously, you may improve outcomes but not learn why. Behavioral work is most valuable when it produces learning you can reuse.

**Measuring only overall improvement and missing equity impacts.**  
A nudge that improves the average completion rate but widens gaps is not a success. Slice results by neighborhood, language, device type, and other relevant dimensions.

**Scaling without operational planning.**  
A successful nudge can increase demand. If the service cannot handle the additional volume, you can create a new bottleneck downstream.

**Ethical missteps.**  
Shame-based messages, coercive framing, or hidden opt-outs may “work” in the narrow sense while undermining trust. Public sector nudges should be designed to respect autonomy and transparency.

Behavioral insights are not magic, and nudges are not shortcuts around policy. They are a practical method for improving public services by designing for humans: limited attention, cognitive load, habits, and defaults. The work starts with service design, identifies behavioral bottlenecks, tests alternatives, and measures outcomes with clear metrics.

Richard Thaler’s core point is still the most useful: choices are always presented in some way, and defaults are rarely neutral. The question is whether governments design that choice environment intentionally and ethically to make services easier to use and more effective.

If you want to start small, pick one high friction local service, an application, a renewal, an appointment process. Map the journey. Identify one bottleneck. Test one redesign. Measure the result. Over time, these “small” changes are how local governments build programs that work better in practice, not just on paper.
